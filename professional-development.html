Professional Development

Professional Development

“Tell me and I forget, teach me and I may remember, involve me and I learn” — Often attributed to Benjamin Franklin, the true origin is debated

Assuming that you have your life’s calling all figured out, it’s time to start preparing yourself for future opportunities.

Think, how can I stand out in a sea of other qualified applicants?

Professional development goes far beyond the classroom. It includes putting yourself in situations where you can gain valuable experience, build meaningful professional relationships, and present yourself as a competitive candidate.

Networking

Speaking as a remote employee, most of my social interaction involves conversing with family, digital communication with co-workers and customers, and asking Alexa to add tasks to my to-do list. In today’s post-pandemic, technology-driven world, it can be more challenging to build authentic and meaningful professional relationships. This places greater importance on students to learn how to network effectively and intentionally.

According to a Gallup survey that asked college graduates which job skills they felt they needed more training in, close to half replied with networking. While colleges may offer career fairs, alumni directories and other networking opportunities, a Times Higher Education article by Julia Freeland Fisher points out that providing access to these resources aren’t enough. The article explains, “if students don’t know how to ask faculty for help, approach prospective employers at a job fair…simply brokering access to employers or alumni will not yield results.” As students, we can take a proactive approach to closing the gap between what is taught in a traditional classroom setting and what is needed to successfully transition into the workforce.

You’re probably thinking, so how can I learn how to network? Whether you’re an introvert or not, it can be intimidating to approach a stranger or attend an event where you don’t know anyone. Start close to home. Practice introducing yourself and having conversations with people you already feel comfortable talking to like a family member, close friend, professor, or counselor. Ask open ended questions that genuinely interest you and relate to the other person’s experiences. The point is to build confidence in starting conversations without overthinking every interaction.

Instead of asking: “What do you do for work?”
Try asking this: “What advice would you give someone interested in working in your field?”

Networking opportunities can exist anywhere. Earlier this year, I attended an industry convention for work and was invited by a few colleagues to meet in the hotel lobby. A slew of professionals from other companies piled in with them to catch the last hour before last call. After a few quick introductions, I was asked “If we were to open a restaurant and you were asked to contribute to the menu a dish that you consider to be your best recipe, what would you make?” It was a fun conversation, and I wasn’t the only one that was asked. Most everyone there had met a few days prior or had known each other for years from working in the industry, but I still felt included. Networking doesn’t always have to be about work or careers. Sometimes it’s simply about getting to know people and allowing yourself to be genuine, and letting professional relationships develop organically.

Interview Prep

MiraCosta’s Interview Resource:

  • Prepare for Your Interview

In addition to MiraCosta’s interview resources, the following tips are based on my own interviewing experiences, both as a candidate and as someone actively involved in the hiring process at my current employment.

  • Before the interview
    • Print and bring a copy of your resume (and cover letter) to the interview
    • Research the company you’re applying for
      • Look up the company website
      • Read the ‘About’ page on the site
      • What are their core values?
      • Look for recent company news or accomplishments
      • Know your audience
        • Who is interviewing you?
        • Is it a panel interview?
        • Do you know the job titles of the interviewers?
        • I was told a story from a previous colleague about a sales pitch gone wrong. The person with the sales pitch completely ignored the top brass/decision maker in the room and only directed his attention to one person instead of addressing the room.
  • Common questions
    • When researching the company, look up common questions asked in that industry
    • Previous candidates may even post interview questions online that can help you better prepare
  • Practice interviewing
    • Ask someone to role play in a mock interview, if unavailable, ask yourself the questions and answer back
    • Some interview questions may ask scenario-based questions, if you cannot come up with a specific scenario, instead, provide an answer of what you would do if you came across that situation
    • If you cannot think of an answer right away or ‘blank out’, ask the interviewer if you can come back to that question later
      • Bring a paper and pen during the interview
      • You could have a question you may want to ask after the interview
    • You may want to incorporate some of the company’s core values in your answers, only if they feel genuine
  • Questions to ask the interviewer
    • Avoid asking questions that can be answered by looking on the company’s website
    • Ask open-ended questions about the role, team, or company unless it’s about salary and benefits
      • If the job posting or interviewer did not provide information about the salary range, benefits (health insurance, 401k, PTO, etc.)
    • Sample questions:
      • What do you enjoy most about working here? (Gives you a chance to get to know the company culture)
      • What qualities do you think makes someone successful in this role? (Provides insight on what skills and traits the hiring manager values the most)
      • What does a typical day look like in this position? (Gives the interviewer a chance to clarify responsibilities)
      • What is your anticipated timeline to fill this position? (Sets expectation of when you can expect to hear back)
    • Choose questions that help you better understand the role, the team, and the hiring manager’s expectations. The goal is to have a genuine conversation outside of their list of standard interview questions. Remember, interviews aren’t just for companies to determine if you’re the right fit, it’s also your opportunity to decide if the company is the right fit for you.
  • Follow-up emails
    • Send a quick thank you email within 24 hours of your interview
      • Thank the interviewer for their time and opportunity
      • Be personal, mention something specific that was discussed during the interview
    • Follow up again only after the timeline discussed during the interview has passed and you have not received an update
